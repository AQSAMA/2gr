# II. LITERATURE REVIEW (SUPPORTIVE CONTEXT)

This chapter synthesizes supportive literature for the present Iraqi cross-sectional study. It focuses on four domains defined in the study framework: stigma and public attitudes, medication beliefs and acceptance, adherence determinants, and Iraqi or regional service realities. The chapter provides interpretive context for the study design and discussion, while primary study results are reported separately in later chapters.

## A. Stigma and Public Attitudes

Evidence from Arab and international literature shows that stigma remains a major barrier to treatment use. Conceptual and empirical work describes stigma as a social process that extends from stereotypes to exclusion and delayed care, with effects at public, institutional, and self-directed levels (Zolezzi et al., 2018; Booth et al., 2024). In many conservative settings across the Arab region, stigma is often tied to family reputation and social honor, so treatment decisions are shaped by community judgment as much as by symptom burden (Booth et al., 2024).

Comparative evidence adds an important contrast. Population surveys show that professional psychiatric help can be viewed positively in principle, yet this does not remove social distance or fear-based attitudes toward people with mental illness (Angermeyer et al., 2017). Arab-focused synthesis similarly shows variation across countries and groups, with persistent beliefs linking mental illness to moral or supernatural explanations in some communities, which can weaken early engagement with biomedical care (Zolezzi et al., 2018).

Iraqi evidence fits this mixed pattern. University data from Baghdad indicates that stigma is still measurable among young adults and is shaped by psychosocial conditions rather than by one single factor (Hussein Alwan et al., 2025). Parallel military-focused stigma literature, while based on a different population, supports the same behavioral direction: stronger stigma is linked to lower help-seeking and delayed treatment contact (Rasheed et al., 2026). Together, these findings suggest that Iraqi anti-stigma work should target both public narratives and personal treatment fears.

## B. Medication Beliefs and Acceptance

Medication acceptance is strongly influenced by how people weigh perceived necessity against perceived risk. The Beliefs about Medicines Questionnaire formalized this balance and showed that concern about dependency, toxicity, and overuse can reduce willingness to continue treatment even when medicines are seen as potentially helpful (Horne et al., 1999). This framework remains useful for psychiatric medication because acceptance decisions are rarely based on efficacy information alone.

Longitudinal public-opinion evidence from the United States shows that acceptance can improve over time, but concern about harms may remain stable even when perceived benefits increase (Mojtabai, 2009). This pattern is relevant to Iraq: awareness campaigns may raise acceptance partly, but persistent fear of side effects or addiction can still limit sustained adherence behavior.

Regional Arab evidence supports this interpretation. In GCC countries, mental health literacy remains uneven and stigma remains common even among educated groups, which can weaken confidence in evidence-based psychiatric treatment (Elyamani et al., 2021). Work among Iraqi refugees in Australia shows a related tension: formal psychiatric support may be accepted by many participants, while religious coping remains highly endorsed and disorder recognition can stay low (Slewa-Younan et al., 2014). For Iraq, these findings suggest that improving acceptance requires both clearer medication communication and culturally aware counseling.

## C. Adherence and Determinants

Adherence literature indicates that psychiatric medication continuity is shaped by interacting social, psychological, and system-level factors rather than by patient motivation alone. Review-level evidence identifies patterns in which social support and socioeconomic conditions may support adherence, while depression, financial barriers, and minority-related disadvantage can reduce it (Gast & Mathes, 2019). This supports a multi-factor interpretation of nonadherence in routine care.

Stigma-related mechanisms add another layer. Evidence from mental illness populations shows that self-stigma is associated with lower adherence and weaker engagement with treatment, partly through shame, low self-efficacy, and withdrawal from care pathways (Abdisa et al., 2020). Intervention evidence in schizophrenia also shows that information-only psychoeducation is usually insufficient; programs with motivational, behavioral, and follow-up support elements are more likely to improve adherence (Zygmunt et al., 2002).

Family context is especially relevant for younger patients. Systematic review evidence indicates that parental beliefs, family functioning, and parental mental health can strongly influence adherence among children and adolescents receiving psychiatric medication (Kalaman et al., 2023). Taken together, the adherence literature points to an Iraqi practice implication: adherence support should combine stigma reduction, practical counseling, and family-aware follow-up instead of relying on single-message education.

## D. Iraqi and Regional Service Realities

Recent Iraqi and regional studies show that medication acceptance is embedded in health-system structure, prescribing practice, and available care pathways. In Baghdad inpatient settings, high-dose antipsychotic prescribing and polypharmacy have been reported at levels that raise safety and quality concerns, indicating a need for stronger guideline-aligned prescribing governance (Nassr & Wadeea, 2026). Among university students in Basra, psychotropic use appears within a broader context of anxiety and depression burden, highlighting early-adult demand for mental health support in academic environments (Kadhim et al., 2024).

Service-use patterns also show parallel systems of care. Iraqi evidence reports frequent recourse to faith healers, with many patients reaching non-medical treatment routes before, during, or after psychiatric consultation (Younis et al., 2020). This reflects both cultural belief systems and practical barriers in formal services. It also means that public acceptance of psychiatric medication cannot be interpreted without considering where people first seek help.

Regional system reviews reinforce this picture. MENA services continue to face workforce limitations, uneven access, and stigma-linked barriers, with rural and low-resource populations carrying a larger treatment gap (Okasha et al., 2025). At the same time, evidence from low- and middle-income settings shows that integrating mental health into primary care can improve outcomes and remain cost-effective when implemented with adequate support (Cubillos et al., 2021). For Iraq, the literature supports a dual direction: improve public acceptance and strengthen service design so accepted treatment is also reachable.
