# V. DISCUSSION

## A. Interpretation of Primary Findings

This cross-sectional survey shows a mixed public position on psychiatric medication in Iraq. Recommendation willingness was higher than acceptance and safety beliefs. A total of 57.65% reported that they would recommend psychiatric medication, while 30.72% said no and 11.62% were not sure. At the same time, only 30.80% considered psychiatric medication acceptable, and only 23.65% considered it safe. This pattern suggests that part of the public supports treatment in principle but still carries personal concern about medication risks.

Belief items support this interpretation. More than half of respondents agreed that doctors may prescribe psychiatric medication more than necessary (Q11, 51.55% agree), and 67.40% agreed that most psychiatric medications can cause psychological or physical dependence (Q12). In parallel, 51.95% agreed that modern psychiatric medications are safer than older ones (Q13). This combination reflects ambivalence rather than simple rejection: concern about overuse and dependence is high, but confidence in newer treatment options is also present.

The primary adjusted logistic model clarifies which factors were most related to recommendation behavior in this dataset. Female gender was associated with higher odds of recommending medication (adjusted OR 1.516, 95% CI 1.043–2.205). Higher Q13 scores were associated with higher recommendation odds (adjusted OR 1.507, 95% CI 1.248–1.820). By contrast, stronger agreement with overprescribing concerns (Q11) was associated with lower recommendation odds (adjusted OR 0.830, 95% CI 0.696–0.991), and fear was also associated with lower recommendation odds (adjusted OR 0.504, 95% CI 0.358–0.711). These findings indicate that benefit-trust beliefs and fear-related concerns move in opposite directions in public acceptance.

The multinomial model that preserved the No/Yes/Not sure response structure showed a similar direction for key predictors. Female gender remained associated with both affirmative and uncertain response patterns relative to refusal, and trust in modern medication safety remained central for affirmative attitudes. This supports the main model interpretation that uncertainty is not random noise; it appears to be a meaningful middle position linked to stigma and trust variables.

The contact hypothesis analysis adds another layer. Participants with prior medication use differed from non-users on core beliefs, but effect sizes were small. Users showed less agreement with overprescribing and dependence statements and stronger agreement with modern medication safety. This pattern is consistent with the idea that personal exposure can reduce some negative beliefs, but it does not remove skepticism fully.

The exploratory stigma phenotype analysis also supports heterogeneity in attitudes. The four-profile solution separated respondents into groups with different combinations of overuse concern, dependence concern, and safety trust. For pharmacy practice, this means one generic message is unlikely to shift all groups equally. Communication strategies should target the specific belief profile that drives resistance.

## B. Comparison With Iraqi and Regional Literature

The survey pattern is broadly consistent with mapped Iraqi and regional evidence showing that stigma, treatment fear, and cultural interpretation continue to shape medication acceptance. In Iraq, Younis et al. (2020) documented high use of faith healers among psychiatric patients, often before psychiatric consultation. In the present survey, high fear-related effects and strong dependence concerns help explain why delayed or alternative care pathways can persist even when many respondents still endorse recommendation in principle.

Mapped regional stigma literature supports this interpretation. Zolezzi et al. (2018) identified wide variation in Arab stigma beliefs but persistent treatment-related stigma across settings. Angermeyer et al. (2017) reported that public support for psychiatric care has improved in many contexts but psychotherapy remains preferred over medication in many populations. Booth et al. (2024) further showed that conservative social settings amplify shame and family-level reputation concerns around mental illness. The Iraqi survey finding of coexistence between recommendation and fear aligns with this broader regional pattern of conditional, not complete, acceptance.

The medication-belief structure in this survey also mirrors the necessity-concern framework described in the Beliefs about Medicines Questionnaire literature (Horne et al., 1999). In our data, confidence in modern medication safety increased recommendation, while overprescribing concern and fear reduced recommendation. This matches the mapped literature view that adherence and acceptance tend to improve when benefit beliefs outweigh concern beliefs.

Mental health literacy evidence from mapped Gulf and Iraqi refugee literature is also relevant. Elyamani et al. (2021) reported limited mental health literacy and persistent stigma across Gulf populations. Slewa-Younan et al. (2014) found low PTSD label recognition among Iraqi refugees but high endorsement of both psychiatric and religious support. The present survey similarly suggests overlap rather than strict opposition between medical and cautious attitudes: respondents can support treatment while still holding strong concern about dependence and prescriber behavior.

Service and prescribing literature from Iraq and the region provides system context for these attitudes. Nassr and Wadeea (2026) reported high-dose antipsychotic exposure and polypharmacy patterns among inpatients in Baghdad. Kadhim et al. (2024) described psychotropic use among Basra medical students with signs of informal and irregular use behavior. Together with our survey findings, these studies suggest that public concern about overuse is not detached from clinical reality. It may reflect real community observation of prescribing complexity and uneven medication counseling.

Finally, integration evidence from Cubillos et al. (2021) and regional service analysis from Okasha et al. (2025) support a practical reading of this survey: acceptance barriers are not only individual beliefs. They are also linked to service design, workforce limits, and uneven integration of mental health into routine care. The survey’s uncertainty group is likely to remain large unless service access, trust-building, and communication quality improve together.

## C. Implications for Pharmacy Practice and Public Health

For Iraqi pharmacy practice, the main implication is that counseling should directly target fear, dependence concern, and overprescribing concern, because these factors were statistically related to recommendation behavior in the primary model. Pharmacists in community and hospital settings are positioned to provide repeated, plain-language risk-benefit counseling and to correct myths about dependence and long-term harm.

The second implication is segmentation. The exploratory phenotype findings and the users versus non-users contrast indicate that public attitudes are not uniform. Public education should use profile-sensitive messages rather than a single generic campaign. Respondents who already trust modern medication safety may need adherence support, while respondents with strong fear and overuse concern need trust-building before adherence strategies can work.

The third implication is interprofessional coordination. If public concern includes doubts about prescribing appropriateness, pharmacists, psychiatrists, and primary care physicians should communicate consistent treatment plans and follow-up logic. This aligns with mapped regional evidence that integrated primary care models can improve mental health outcomes when behavioral health is embedded into routine services (Cubillos et al., 2021).

At public health level, the survey supports a dual-track strategy. One track should improve medication literacy in the general population, especially around safety modernization and dependence risk communication. The second track should reduce stigma-related fear through culturally grounded communication that is compatible with Iraqi social and religious context, as recommended by mapped regional stigma literature (Booth et al., 2024; Zolezzi et al., 2018).

## D. Strengths and Limitations of the Present Study

A major strength is the analytic structure of the results. The study did not rely on one model only; it used hierarchical logistic modeling, multinomial modeling that preserved uncertainty responses, contact comparison, and exploratory profiling. This gave a richer view of acceptance than binary summaries alone.

Another strength is sample size and internal consistency across analyses. The survey included 877 respondents, and key directional findings were repeated across primary and multinomial models, which increases interpretive confidence for the main belief-related predictors.

The study also has clear limitations. The design is cross-sectional, so associations cannot establish temporal or causal direction. Self-reported responses can be affected by social desirability and recall effects. The sample was also demographically imbalanced toward younger and female participants, which may limit generalization to the full Iraqi adult population.

Model-level limitations should also be stated. Complete-case analysis reduced sample size in adjusted models compared with the full descriptive dataset. The primary model had modest explanatory power, which indicates that relevant predictors were not fully captured. Exploratory stigma phenotypes are hypothesis-generating and should not be treated as confirmatory subgroup taxonomy without external validation.

## E. Priority Research Gaps

Future Iraqi research should test causal pathways using longitudinal or repeated cross-sectional designs to track whether belief change leads to behavior change. This is needed because the present design identifies associations but cannot show sequence.

A second gap is measurement expansion. Future studies should include broader trust, stigma, and service-experience variables so that explanatory models capture more of the variance in acceptance outcomes.

A third gap is representativeness. Multi-governorate sampling with stronger age and gender balance is needed to estimate national patterns more accurately and to examine urban-rural differences.

A fourth gap is intervention testing. Based on the present findings, pharmacist-led counseling interventions and profile-targeted communication packages should be tested in pragmatic trials, with outcomes that include recommendation, acceptance, adherence intention, and help-seeking behavior.

A fifth gap is service linkage research. Given mapped evidence on integration and workforce constraints, implementation studies should test how primary care integration and pharmacist-physician coordination affect medication trust and uptake in Iraq.
