# IV. DISCUSSION

## A. Principal Findings from the Survey

This study provides original cross-sectional evidence that recommendation willingness for psychiatric medications in Iraq is shaped mainly by belief and fear variables rather than demographic variables alone. The hierarchical model sequence showed that demographics had minimal explanatory value, while adding belief and fear variables produced a clear gain in fit. In adjusted analysis, agreement that modern medications are safer was linked to higher recommendation willingness, while fear and stronger overprescribing concerns were linked to lower willingness.

The multinomial analysis adds an important point that binary models cannot show clearly: hesitation is a distinct response state. Some predictors that were relevant for clear recommendation did not remain relevant for "not sure," which means acceptance campaigns should not treat hesitant respondents as a simple midpoint between acceptance and rejection.

## B. Interpretation in Iraqi Context

The findings are consistent with the Iraqi context where trust, fear, and stigma-related beliefs influence medication behavior. Earlier Iraqi evidence showed high stigma and low confidence in mental health services (Sadik et al., 2010), and the present survey pattern supports the same direction: concerns about overprescribing and medication harms remain central obstacles. At the same time, positive safety beliefs were strongly associated with recommendation behavior, which identifies a concrete communication target for pharmacists and clinicians.

The gender association observed in both primary and multinomial models should be interpreted carefully as an association, not causation. This caution is especially relevant because the study sample was not gender-balanced (71.95% female and 28.05% male). The association may reflect social roles, care-seeking patterns, or differential exposure to medication narratives, and should be examined in follow-up studies with more balanced gender distribution and stratified sampling.

## C. Comparison with Supportive Literature

Regional literature from Gulf and wider Arab settings has repeatedly shown mixed mental health literacy and persistent concerns about dependence and stigma (Elyamani et al., 2021; Booth et al., 2024). The present results align with that pattern but add Iraq-specific quantitative estimates linking these beliefs to recommendation willingness.

International work on beliefs about medicines has shown that higher concern and lower necessity beliefs reduce adherence and acceptance. The current findings are coherent with that framework because concern-oriented belief constructs moved in the expected direction, while safety-oriented belief constructs moved toward acceptance.

## D. Implications for Pharmacy Practice and Public Health

For Iraqi pharmacy practice, the results support targeted counseling that directly addresses fear, overprescribing concern, and safety misunderstanding instead of generic information delivery. The recommendation gap between prior users and non-users also suggests that informed, structured contact with treatment may reduce resistance over time.

For public communication, a segmented approach is needed. People with rejection-oriented beliefs need risk-benefit clarification and trust-building messages, while hesitant groups need practical, low-pressure counseling that addresses uncertainty without stigma.

## E. Strengths and Limitations

A major strength is the use of a relatively large dataset with complementary model families and explicit separation between main and exploratory analyses. This improves interpretive transparency and reduces mixing of confirmatory and hypothesis-generating findings.

Limitations include the cross-sectional design, which prevents causal inference, reliance on self-reported responses, unequal demographic distribution in the sample, and complete-case reduction in some models. In addition, instrument and ethical governance details should be fully documented in the final submitted thesis package.

## F. Future Research

Future Iraqi studies should use broader geographic sampling, include stronger balance across key demographic strata, and test intervention-focused designs that measure whether targeted counseling can shift fear and safety beliefs. Longitudinal and mixed-methods studies will be useful to clarify how belief change relates to sustained acceptance and real medication behavior over time.
