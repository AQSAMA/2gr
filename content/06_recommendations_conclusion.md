# VI. RECOMMENDATIONS

## A. Policy-Level Recommendations

National and governorate mental health policy should prioritize public medication trust as a measurable target, not only service availability. The survey showed a gap between recommendation willingness and personal safety acceptance, with high concern about dependence and overprescribing. Policy communication frameworks should therefore include standardized, evidence-based public messages on psychiatric medication safety, dependence risk, and treatment monitoring.

Medication governance should also strengthen rational psychotropic prescribing oversight in hospital and outpatient settings. This recommendation is supported by survey concern patterns and by mapped Iraqi prescribing evidence showing high-dose and polypharmacy exposure in inpatient practice (Nassr & Wadeea, 2026). The aim is to reduce avoidable concern by improving prescribing transparency and consistency with guideline-based care.

Policy should expand integrated mental health delivery in primary care, especially in underserved areas, because acceptance barriers and access barriers interact. Mapped evidence from low- and middle-income settings shows that integrated models can improve outcomes and are feasible when adapted to local capacity (Cubillos et al., 2021; Okasha et al., 2025). Integration should include clear pharmacist roles in counseling and follow-up.

## B. Practice-Level Recommendations (Pharmacists, Physicians, Primary Care)

Pharmacists should deliver structured counseling at dispensing and refill points focused on the exact beliefs linked to lower acceptance in this survey: fear, overprescribing concerns, and dependence concerns. Counseling should explain expected benefits, side-effect management, treatment duration logic, and when to seek follow-up.

Physicians and pharmacists should use shared communication scripts for first-line psychiatric medication conversations so patients receive consistent messages across settings. The present findings suggest that mixed messages may reinforce the uncertainty group and reduce trust. Practice standards should require documenting the main patient concern domain at each encounter and addressing it directly.

Primary care teams should establish referral and feedback pathways with psychiatric services and community pharmacies. Since users and non-users differed on core beliefs in the contact analysis, repeated contact with informed providers can be used as a trust-building mechanism. Brief follow-up calls or visits in early treatment weeks can be used to prevent drop-off driven by fear or misinformation.

## C. Education and Awareness Recommendations

Public education campaigns should move from general stigma messaging to belief-targeted content. Campaigns should directly address dependence myths, overprescribing concerns, and safety misconceptions, because these were central predictors in the primary model. Messages should be plain, culturally grounded, and suitable for different educational levels.

University and health college programs should include practical psychiatric medication literacy modules. This recommendation is supported by mapped Iraqi student literature showing psychotropic use patterns and non-ideal use behaviors in medical-group students (Kadhim et al., 2024). Early professional training can improve future provider communication and reduce stigma transfer into practice.

Community awareness work should include collaboration with trusted social actors, including religious and local community leaders, to improve reach in conservative settings. Mapped literature from Iraq and the region shows that help-seeking pathways often include non-medical actors and that culturally compatible communication improves acceptance potential (Younis et al., 2020; Booth et al., 2024).

## D. Research Recommendations

Future studies should test pharmacist-led and team-based interventions that directly target fear and medication-belief barriers identified in this survey. Trials should use pre-specified outcomes that include recommendation behavior, medication acceptance, adherence intention, and sustained engagement with care.

Nationally representative and longitudinal research is needed to validate subgroup patterns and examine belief change over time. Future modeling should include broader service-experience and trust variables and should validate exploratory stigma phenotypes with confirmatory methods.

Implementation research should compare integrated primary care models across Iraqi settings to identify which delivery structure best improves both access and medication trust, using local cost and workforce constraints as core design factors.

# VII. CONCLUSION

## A. What the Cross-Sectional Study Adds

This study adds new Iraq-specific cross-sectional evidence on psychiatric medication acceptance using a large survey and a layered analytic approach. It shows that public attitudes are not purely supportive or purely rejecting. Recommendation willingness is relatively high, but safety acceptance is lower, and concern beliefs remain strong. The study also identifies specific belief domains that are statistically linked to recommendation behavior, especially fear, overprescribing concern, and trust in modern medication safety.

## B. What the Combined Evidence (Survey + Literature) Indicates for Iraq

When interpreted with mapped Iraqi and regional literature, the survey indicates that medication acceptance in Iraq is shaped by both belief-level and system-level factors. Stigma and fear remain active, but they coexist with openness to treatment. Service structure, prescribing context, and communication quality influence whether this openness converts into sustained acceptance. The combined evidence supports integrated care, pharmacist-centered counseling, and culturally compatible public communication as practical directions for improvement.

## C. Closing Evidence-Based Statement

Psychiatric medication acceptance in Iraq can improve when health services address trust and fear directly, not indirectly. The present survey and mapped literature point to one clear direction: align rational prescribing, clear counseling, and culturally grounded public education within integrated care pathways. This approach is realistic for Iraq and can narrow the gap between willingness to recommend treatment and confidence to accept it.
