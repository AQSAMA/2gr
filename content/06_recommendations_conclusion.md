# V. CONCLUSION

## A. Study Conclusion

This original cross-sectional study shows that public acceptance of psychiatric medications in Iraq is strongly associated with modifiable belief and fear constructs. In this sample, recommendation willingness was more sensitive to attitudes about safety and prescribing trust than to most demographic variables. Modeling hesitation separately also showed that uncertainty is a meaningful response state with distinct predictor behavior.

These findings add local quantitative evidence that can be used directly in pharmacy counseling, mental health communication design, and service planning.

## B. Practical Recommendations from Study Findings

For pharmacy practice, counseling should prioritize three messages: realistic safety communication, clear explanation of prescribing decisions, and structured response to dependence fears. For service planners, communication pathways should include specific strategies for hesitant groups rather than binary accept/reject framing. For policy stakeholders, integrating pharmacist-delivered medication counseling into mental health service pathways can improve reach in settings where specialist access remains limited.

## C. Final Perspective

The main contribution of this study is not only that acceptance levels were measured, but that actionable predictors were identified using multivariable analysis on Iraqi primary data. This provides a stronger empirical base for targeted interventions and supports a shift from broad stigma narratives to measurable, testable communication and care strategies.
