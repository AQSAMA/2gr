# I. INTRODUCTION

## A. Background and Context

Iraq has experienced long-term conflict, repeated displacement, and prolonged pressure on health services. This history has contributed to a high burden of mental disorders and a persistent treatment gap (Saied et al., 2023; Younis & Khunda, 2020). At the same time, psychiatric workforce capacity remains low, which limits specialist access and places greater responsibility on primary care and pharmacy contact points. In this context, medication access alone is not enough. Public acceptance of psychiatric medications, trust in their safety, and social beliefs about mental illness influence whether people accept treatment and whether families support treatment pathways.

Iraqi and regional literature shows that stigma, fear of dependence, and mixed explanatory beliefs can reduce care-seeking and delay professional treatment (Sadik et al., 2010; Elyamani et al., 2021; Booth et al., 2024). Most available Iraqi evidence before this project focused on specific clinical sites, student populations, or literature syntheses. Population-level primary analysis centered on medication recommendation and acceptance patterns has remained limited.

## B. Problem Statement

The key problem is not only mental illness prevalence but the social and cognitive barriers that shape treatment decisions. Without local quantitative evidence on how safety beliefs, overprescribing concerns, dependence concerns, prior medication exposure, and fear relate to recommendation behavior, policy and counseling interventions remain broad and weakly targeted.

## C. Study Objectives

This cross-sectional study aimed to quantify willingness to recommend psychiatric medications, identify predictors of recommendation behavior using multivariable models, preserve and interpret response hesitation through multinomial modeling, and generate exploratory insights that can guide future intervention-focused studies.

## D. Research Questions

This study asks whether demographic characteristics, prior medication exposure, and belief variables are associated with recommendation willingness; whether the same predictors behave differently when "not sure" is modeled as a distinct outcome; and which attitudinal constructs appear most relevant for intervention planning in Iraqi pharmacy and public-health settings.

## E. Study Significance

This study contributes original Iraqi survey evidence that can support pharmacist counseling protocols, anti-stigma communication planning, and service design decisions. It also links statistical findings with practical decision points for healthcare professionals who work in high-demand, low-specialist environments.
