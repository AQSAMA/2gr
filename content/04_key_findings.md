# III. RESULTS

## A. Sample Characteristics

The dataset included 877 participants. Females represented 71.95% and males 28.05%. Most respondents were aged 18-25 years (74.57%), followed by 26-35 years (17.53%). University education was the dominant educational category (78.92%), and 78.44% of participants were single.

Model-specific complete-case samples differed by variable availability. The hierarchical logistic models used 647 participants, the multinomial model used 837 participants, and the sensitivity model with additional proximal belief variables used 406 participants.

## B. Descriptive Attitude and Belief Outcomes

Recommendation willingness showed a mixed but generally favorable pattern. Overall, 57.65% reported "Yes" to recommendation willingness, 30.72% reported "No," and 11.62% reported "Not sure." In the same dataset, 51.49% rated psychiatric medications as not acceptable, while 30.80% rated them acceptable and 17.70% were unsure.

Safety concerns were prominent. Only 23.65% perceived psychiatric medications as safe, while 45.12% perceived them as not safe and 31.23% were unsure. Social concern responses also showed substantial concern, with 37.77% "Yes," 13.09% "Not sure," and 49.14% "No" depending on item direction in the instrument coding.

For core belief items, agreement levels were high for concerns and mixed for safety optimism: Q11 agreement 51.55%, Q12 agreement 67.40%, and Q13 agreement 51.95%.

## C. Primary Hierarchical Logistic Regression (Main/Clean)

The hierarchical sequence showed limited explanatory value for demographics alone, moderate increase after adding prior use, and a clear improvement after adding belief and fear variables. McFadden pseudo R² increased from 0.0040 in Block 1 to 0.0116 in Block 2 and to 0.0686 in Block 3. The Block 3 likelihood-ratio test was statistically significant (p<0.0001).

In the final primary block, significant predictors were gender, Q11 (overprescribing concern), Q13 (modern medication safety belief), and fear. Female gender was positively associated with recommendation willingness (AOR 1.516, 95% CI 1.043 to 2.205, p=0.0294). Higher Q11 agreement was negatively associated with recommendation (AOR 0.830, 95% CI 0.696 to 0.991, p=0.0396). Higher Q13 agreement was positively associated with recommendation (AOR 1.507, 95% CI 1.248 to 1.820, p<0.0001). Fear was negatively associated with recommendation (AOR 0.504, 95% CI 0.358 to 0.711, p<0.0001).

Age, education, marital status, prior use, and Q12 were not statistically significant in this final adjusted model.

## D. Sensitivity Model (Reported Separately)

A sensitivity model that added proximal beliefs Q6 and Q7 used a smaller complete-case sample (n=406) and yielded a substantially higher pseudo R² (0.2440). This model was separated from the main model family because Q6 and Q7 are conceptually close to recommendation content and can dominate explained variance.

## E. Multinomial Logistic Regression (Main/Clean)

The multinomial model preserved the three-level response structure (No/Yes/Not sure) and used 837 complete cases. For the "Yes" equation, gender (RRR 1.558, p=0.0133), Q12 (RRR 0.794, p=0.0239), and Q13 (RRR 1.585, p<0.0001) were significant. For the "Not sure" equation, only gender remained significant (RRR 2.171, p=0.0093), while Q13 was not significant.

These findings indicate that predictors of clear acceptance differ partly from predictors of hesitation, and that modeling hesitation as its own category provides additional interpretive value.

## F. Exploratory Analyses (Secondary)

### 1. Contact Hypothesis Comparisons

Among respondents with prior use exposure (n=127) versus non-users (n=716), users showed lower concern-oriented medians on Q11 (overprescribing concern) and Q12 (dependence concern), and higher safety-oriented median on Q13 (modern medication safety belief). Mann-Whitney tests were significant for Q11 (p=0.0428), Q12 (p=0.0317), and Q13 (p=0.0002), with the strongest directional difference observed for Q13.

### 2. Exploratory Belief Profiles

K-means clustering on standardized Q11-Q13 selected k=4 by the highest silhouette score (0.303). The four profiles showed distinct combinations of prescribing concern, dependence concern, and safety beliefs, supporting heterogeneity in attitudinal patterns within the sample.

## G. Correlation and Group Pattern Notes

Correlation analysis showed modest relationships among belief and acceptance variables. Recommendation correlated positively with acceptance (r=0.232) and weakly with Q13 (r=0.077), while showing weak negative correlation with Q11 (r=-0.086) and Q12 (r=-0.087). Recommendation was higher among prior users (65.08%) than non-users (55.80%).
