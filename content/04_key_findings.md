# IV. RESULTS

## A. Sample Profile and Descriptive Statistics

The survey included 877 respondents. The sample was predominantly female (71.95%) and young, with 74.57% aged 18–25 years. University-level education was common (78.92%), and 78.44% were single. These characteristics describe the respondent profile used for all downstream analyses.

## B. Main Outcome Distributions

Recommendation willingness toward psychiatric medication (Q8) was distributed as 57.65% yes, 11.62% not sure, and 30.72% no. For related attitude items, safety perception was 23.65% yes, 31.23% not sure, and 45.12% no, while acceptability was 30.80% yes, 17.70% not sure, and 51.49% no. These distributions show that recommendation willingness was higher than direct safety or acceptability endorsement.

## C. Hierarchical Logistic Regression (Primary Model)

The primary hierarchical logistic analysis used complete-case n=647. Model fit improved across blocks, with the largest improvement after adding belief variables. In the final adjusted model, female gender was positively associated with recommendation willingness (adjusted OR 1.516, 95% CI 1.043 to 2.205, p=0.0294). Greater agreement that modern medications are safer (Q13) was also positively associated (adjusted OR 1.507, 95% CI 1.248 to 1.820, p<0.0001). Fear-related concern was negatively associated with recommendation willingness (adjusted OR 0.504, 95% CI 0.358 to 0.711, p<0.0001). Q11 showed a smaller negative association (adjusted OR 0.830, p=0.0396).

## D. Multinomial Logistic Regression (No/Yes/Not Sure Structure)

The multinomial model preserved the three response categories and used complete-case n=837. Gender remained a significant discriminator in both non-reference equations. Q13 remained positively associated with a favorable recommendation profile in the model structure, while Q12 showed a significant inverse association in one equation. This model confirmed that key belief variables and gender influenced both endorsement and hesitation patterns.

## E. The Contact Hypothesis (Users vs Non-Users)

Users (n=127) and non-users (n=716) were compared across core belief items. Users showed modestly lower agreement with overprescribing and dependence statements and higher agreement with modern medication safety. Mann–Whitney tests were significant for Q11 (p=0.0428), Q12 (p=0.0317), and Q13 (p=0.0002), with small-to-moderate effect sizes.

## F. Exploratory Stigma Phenotypes (Clearly Labeled)

Exploratory clustering on standardized Q11–Q13 responses selected a four-profile solution by silhouette score. The four groups showed distinct combinations of concern and safety beliefs, indicating heterogeneity in stigma-related response patterns beyond single-item summaries.

## G. Results Summary

Across models and descriptive outcomes, recommendation willingness was most strongly associated with treatment-belief structure rather than broad demographic differences. Confidence in modern medication safety and lower fear-based concern were consistent correlates of favorable recommendation responses, while user versus non-user comparisons supported the contact-hypothesis pattern.
