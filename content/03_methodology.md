# III. METHODOLOGY (ORIGINAL CROSS-SECTIONAL STUDY)

## A. Study Design and Setting

This project used an original cross-sectional study design. The analysis was conducted on the finalized project survey dataset for psychiatric medication use and public acceptance in Iraq.

## B. Participants and Sampling

The analytical dataset contained 877 completed responses. The study used a non-probability survey sample of respondents from Iraq. For model-based analyses requiring complete data across selected covariates and belief items, complete-case samples were used.

## C. Survey Instrument

The questionnaire collected demographic characteristics, general treatment attitudes, and medication belief items. Core constructs included recommendation willingness, acceptance, safety perception, concern-related responses, and prior medication use. Key belief items used in the main models included Q11, Q12, Q13, and prior-use item Q31.

## D. Variables and Operational Definitions

The primary binary outcome was recommendation willingness for psychiatric medication (Q8 yes versus no). A multinomial outcome version preserved response structure (no, yes, not sure). Predictors included age, gender, education, marital status, prior use, and belief items. Model-ready recoded variables included Age_Binary, Gender_Binary, Edu_Binary, Married_Binary, PriorUse_Binary, and Fear_Binary.

## E. Data Management and Statistical Analysis

Data were cleaned and recoded before analysis. Main inferential analyses followed the approved model sequence: hierarchical block logistic regression for the primary binary outcome and multinomial logistic regression for the three-category recommendation outcome. The hierarchical logistic model used complete-case n=647, and the multinomial model used complete-case n=837. Adjusted odds ratios or relative risk ratios with 95% confidence intervals and p-values were reported. The contact hypothesis analysis compared users versus non-users with Mann–Whitney U tests and Cliff’s delta effect sizes. Exploratory stigma phenotype analysis used k-means clustering on standardized Q11–Q13 items.

## F. Ethical Considerations

The analysis used an anonymized survey dataset with no direct personal identifiers in the analytical file. Participation was voluntary within the source survey process.

## G. Methodological Limitations

As a cross-sectional design, this study identifies associations but cannot establish causality. The non-probability sample limits national representativeness. Findings may also be affected by self-report bias and item-level missingness that reduced complete-case sample sizes in regression models.
