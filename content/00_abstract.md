# ABSTRACT

This study examined psychiatric medication use and public acceptance in Iraq using an original cross-sectional survey dataset. The analysis included 877 respondents and assessed treatment attitudes, medication beliefs, and recommendation behavior.

Descriptive results showed a young sample profile, with 74.57% aged 18–25 years and 71.95% female. Recommendation willingness toward psychiatric medication was 57.65% yes, 11.62% not sure, and 30.72% no. Belief items also showed mixed attitudes, with substantial agreement that doctors may overprescribe and that dependence can occur, alongside moderate agreement that modern medications are safer.

In the primary hierarchical logistic model (complete-case n=647), female gender (adjusted OR 1.516, p=0.0294), stronger belief in modern medication safety (Q13; adjusted OR 1.507, p<0.0001), and lower fear-based concern (Fear_Binary; adjusted OR 0.504, p<0.0001) were independently associated with recommendation willingness. In the multinomial model (n=837), gender and key belief variables remained important for distinguishing yes and hesitant response categories.

These findings show that public acceptance of psychiatric medication in Iraq is shaped by a combination of stigma-related beliefs and treatment confidence rather than demographic factors alone. The results support pharmacist-led counseling, targeted public communication on medication safety and dependence concerns, and stigma-aware community strategies to improve treatment acceptance.
