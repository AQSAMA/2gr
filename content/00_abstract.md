# ABSTRACT

Background: Public acceptance of psychiatric medication in Iraq affects treatment uptake, adherence, and early help-seeking, yet current evidence is limited. This study examined psychiatric medication use attitudes and recommendation behavior in an Iraqi cross-sectional sample and interpreted findings against mapped Iraqi and regional literature.

Methods: A cross-sectional survey (N = 877) was analyzed using descriptive statistics, hierarchical logistic regression for recommendation behavior, multinomial logistic regression preserving No/Yes/Not sure responses, a users versus non-users contact analysis, and exploratory stigma phenotype profiling. Primary quantitative results were derived from the analyzed survey dataset.

Results: Recommendation willingness was 57.65% yes, 11.62% not sure, and 30.72% no. Safety perception was lower (23.65% yes, 31.23% not sure, 45.12% no), and acceptability was 30.80% yes, 17.70% not sure, and 51.49% no. In the primary adjusted model, female gender (adjusted OR 1.516, 95% CI 1.043–2.205) and stronger belief that modern medications are safer (Q13; adjusted OR 1.507, 95% CI 1.248–1.820) were associated with higher recommendation odds. Overprescribing concern (Q11; adjusted OR 0.830, 95% CI 0.696–0.991) and fear (adjusted OR 0.504, 95% CI 0.358–0.711) were associated with lower recommendation odds. Prior users showed more favorable core belief patterns than non-users, with small effect sizes.

Implications: Psychiatric medication acceptance in Iraq appears ambivalent rather than uniformly negative. Findings support pharmacist-led counseling on fear and dependence concerns, coordinated prescriber-pharmacist communication, and culturally grounded mental health literacy strategies within integrated primary care pathways.
