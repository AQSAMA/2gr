# ABSTRACT

Psychiatric medication acceptance in Iraq remains a public health issue because demand for care is rising while stigma and treatment hesitation persist. This study examined psychiatric medication use and public acceptance in Iraq using an original cross-sectional survey and literature review. We analyzed responses from 877 participants. Main outcomes included safety perception, acceptability, recommendation willingness, and social concern. The analysis used descriptive statistics, hierarchical logistic regression, multinomial logistic regression, and exploratory Users vs Non-Users and stigma-phenotype analyses.

The sample was mostly female (71.95%), aged 18–25 years (74.57%), and university educated (78.92%). Recommendation willingness was 57.65%, but only 23.65% perceived psychiatric medication as safe and 30.80% considered it acceptable; 45.12% viewed it as unsafe and 51.49% as unacceptable. In the primary hierarchical model (complete-case n=647), recommendation was positively associated with female gender (adjusted OR 1.516, p=0.0294) and belief that modern medications are safer than older ones (Q13: adjusted OR 1.507, p<0.0001). Recommendation was negatively associated with fear (adjusted OR 0.504, p<0.0001) and belief that doctors prescribe more than necessary (Q11: adjusted OR 0.830, p=0.0396). In multinomial analysis (n=837), dependence concern (Q12) reduced the probability of a “Yes” recommendation (RRR 0.794, p=0.0239). Users reported more favorable medication beliefs than non-users, especially on modern medication safety.

These findings indicate that Iraqi public attitudes are ambivalent rather than uniformly negative. The strongest barriers are fear, mistrust, and dependence concerns, not only demographic factors. Compared with Iraqi and regional literature, the results suggest a persistent trust-and-literacy gap that affects treatment uptake. Policy and practice should prioritize pharmacist-led counseling, integrated primary mental health care, and culturally grounded anti-stigma communication to improve safe medication acceptance and narrow Iraq’s treatment gap.
